# Hedging and Academic Stance

Use this file when revising claims that sound too strong, too subjective, too casual, or insufficiently evidence-based.

## General principles

Academic writing should usually avoid unsupported certainty. Match the strength of the claim to the strength of the evidence. Do not weaken every sentence; hedge only where causality, generalizability, interpretation, or mechanism is uncertain.

## Common hedging functions

### Possibility

Use when the claim is plausible but not conclusively demonstrated.

Preferred expressions:
- may
- might
- could
- may be associated with
- may help explain
- may contribute to
- appears to
- seems to

Examples:
- These findings may help explain the variation in student engagement across institutions.
- The pattern appears to be more pronounced among first-year students.

### Suggestive interpretation

Use when interpreting results, especially in discussion sections.

Preferred expressions:
- suggest that
- indicate that
- imply that
- provide evidence that
- are consistent with
- point to the possibility that

Usage distinctions:
- "show" is stronger than "suggest."
- "demonstrate" is strong and should be reserved for direct evidence.
- "prove" is usually inappropriate in empirical academic writing.

Examples:
- The results suggest that feedback quality may be an important predictor of engagement.
- These findings are consistent with prior research on self-regulated learning.

### Limited generalization

Use when findings may not apply universally.

Preferred expressions:
- in this context
- among the participants in this study
- within the present sample
- under these conditions
- for this population
- to some extent

Examples:
- Within the present sample, perceived support was positively associated with persistence.
- These results may be most relevant to institutions with similar advising structures.

### Causal caution

Use when the study design is not experimental or does not establish causality.

Preferred expressions:
- is associated with
- is related to
- predicts
- is linked to
- may contribute to
- may be explained by

Avoid unless warranted:
- causes
- leads to
- results in
- proves that

Examples:
- Feedback frequency was associated with higher engagement, but the analysis does not establish causality.
- The observed relationship may be partly explained by differences in prior academic preparation.

## Strengthening claims appropriately

Use stronger expressions only when evidence is direct, replicated, or strongly supported.

Preferred expressions:
- provides strong evidence that
- consistently indicates that
- demonstrates that
- confirms that
- supports the conclusion that

Examples:
- The robustness checks provide strong evidence that the association is not driven by model specification.
- The experimental results demonstrate that the intervention improved task completion rates.

## Neutral stance expressions

Use when presenting claims without personal judgment.

Preferred expressions:
- The evidence suggests that...
- The findings indicate that...
- This result is consistent with...
- One possible interpretation is that...
- This pattern may reflect...
- The analysis provides support for...

Avoid:
- I believe
- I think
- It is obvious that
- Everyone knows that
- This is very important, without explanation.

## Common revision patterns

Too strong:
- This proves that online learning improves student outcomes.

Academic revision:
- This suggests that online learning may improve student outcomes under certain conditions.

Too subjective:
- This is a very good method for solving the problem.

Academic revision:
- This method provides a useful approach to addressing the problem, particularly when the available data are limited.

Too causal:
- Teacher feedback caused students to become more engaged.

Academic revision:
- Teacher feedback was positively associated with student engagement.

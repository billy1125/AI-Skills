# Results and Discussion Language

Use this file when revising results, findings, discussion, implications, or limitations paragraphs.

## Reporting results

Preferred expressions:
- The results indicate that...
- The findings suggest that...
- The analysis reveals that...
- The model shows that...
- The coefficient remained significant after...
- No significant association was found between...
- X was positively associated with Y.
- X was negatively associated with Y.

Examples:
- The results indicate that feedback quality was positively associated with student engagement.
- The coefficient remained significant after controlling for demographic characteristics.

## Describing statistical relationships

Use precise language that matches the analysis.

Associational language:
- is associated with
- is related to
- predicts
- is correlated with
- varies with

Causal language:
- affects
- influences
- leads to
- causes

Use causal language only when the design supports causal inference.

Examples:
- Feedback quality was associated with higher levels of engagement.
- The intervention increased task completion rates among participants in the treatment group.

## Interpreting findings

Preferred expressions:
- One possible interpretation is that...
- This finding may reflect...
- This pattern may be explained by...
- These results suggest that...
- This may indicate that...
- A plausible explanation is that...

Examples:
- One possible interpretation is that timely feedback increases students' sense of academic support.
- This pattern may reflect differences in institutional resources across departments.

## Comparing findings with prior literature

Preferred expressions:
- This finding is consistent with...
- This result aligns with...
- These findings extend prior research by...
- This differs from previous studies that...
- In contrast to earlier findings, this study suggests that...

Examples:
- This finding is consistent with prior research showing that instructional support is linked to student persistence.
- These results extend prior research by showing that feedback quality remains relevant after accounting for prior achievement.

## Discussing implications

Preferred expressions:
- These findings have implications for...
- The results suggest that institutions may need to...
- This evidence points to the importance of...
- From a practical perspective,...
- From a theoretical perspective,...

Examples:
- These findings have implications for the design of academic advising programs.
- From a practical perspective, the results suggest that institutions may benefit from improving feedback mechanisms.

## Limitations

Preferred expressions:
- This study has several limitations.
- First, the sample was limited to...
- Second, the cross-sectional design limits causal interpretation.
- Third, the measures relied on self-reported data.
- These limitations should be considered when interpreting the findings.

Examples:
- The cross-sectional design limits the extent to which causal conclusions can be drawn.
- Because the sample was drawn from a single institution, the findings may not be generalizable to other contexts.

## Future research

Preferred expressions:
- Future research should examine...
- Further studies are needed to determine...
- Longitudinal research could clarify...
- Future work may explore...
- Additional research is needed to test whether...

Examples:
- Future research should examine whether these patterns hold across different institutional contexts.
- Longitudinal studies could clarify whether feedback quality predicts changes in engagement over time.

## Diagnosis criteria for results and discussion paragraphs

Check whether the paragraph:
- separates results from interpretation when needed
- avoids causal overclaiming
- connects findings to the research question
- compares results with prior literature
- states limitations precisely
- avoids unsupported practical recommendations

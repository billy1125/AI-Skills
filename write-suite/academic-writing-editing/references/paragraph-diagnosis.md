# Paragraph Diagnosis Checklist

Use this file when the user asks for paragraph diagnosis, logic review, academic writing feedback, or explanation of why a paragraph does or does not work.

## Diagnostic output structure

When diagnosing a paragraph, use this structure unless the user requests another format:

1. Overall assessment
2. Main issue
3. Sentence-level diagnosis
4. Suggested revision strategy
5. Revised version, if requested or clearly useful

Keep feedback neutral and specific. Do not overextend beyond the text provided.

## Core criteria

### 1. Topic sentence clarity

Check whether the paragraph begins with a clear controlling idea. A strong academic paragraph usually makes its function visible early.

Problems:
- The paragraph begins with details before presenting the main point.
- The first sentence is too broad.
- The topic sentence does not match the evidence that follows.

Diagnosis language:
- The paragraph has a clear topic sentence, but the subsequent evidence does not fully develop it.
- The opening sentence introduces the general topic, but it does not yet establish the paragraph's specific claim.
- The paragraph would be stronger if the first sentence stated the analytical point more directly.

### 2. Logical progression

Check whether each sentence follows from the previous one.

Problems:
- Sentences are adjacent but not logically connected.
- The paragraph jumps from background to conclusion without explanation.
- A contrast or causal relation is implied but not marked.

Diagnosis language:
- The logical connection between these two sentences needs to be made more explicit.
- The paragraph moves from evidence to interpretation too quickly.
- The transition suggests addition, but the actual relation is contrast.

### 3. Evidence and support

Check whether claims are supported by evidence, examples, citations, or reasoning.

Problems:
- The paragraph makes a broad claim without support.
- The evidence is listed but not interpreted.
- The citation is present, but its relevance is not explained.

Diagnosis language:
- The paragraph presents evidence, but the analytical significance of that evidence remains underdeveloped.
- This claim requires either a citation or a more specific explanation.
- The cited study is mentioned, but the paragraph does not explain how it supports the argument.

### 4. Academic tone

Check whether the paragraph uses formal, neutral, and cautious language.

Problems:
- Overly strong claims.
- Conversational wording.
- Subjective judgment.
- Emotional or evaluative phrasing.

Diagnosis language:
- The tone is generally academic, but several claims would benefit from more cautious phrasing.
- The wording here is somewhat conversational and could be revised into a more formal register.
- The claim is plausible, but it is stated more strongly than the evidence appears to support.

### 5. Coherence and cohesion

Check whether pronouns, repeated terms, and transitions guide the reader.

Problems:
- Too many repeated nouns.
- Ambiguous pronoun reference.
- Missing signposting.
- Excessive connectors.

Diagnosis language:
- The paragraph is coherent at the sentence level, but the relationship between the examples could be signposted more clearly.
- The pronoun reference is ambiguous and may confuse the reader.
- The paragraph uses several transitions, but they do not all reflect the actual logical relation.

### 6. Unity

Check whether all sentences serve the same paragraph-level purpose.

Problems:
- A sentence introduces a new topic that belongs in another paragraph.
- The paragraph tries to make multiple claims.
- The conclusion does not follow from the opening point.

Diagnosis language:
- The paragraph contains two distinct ideas and may be clearer if divided into two paragraphs.
- This sentence introduces a new issue that is not developed in the rest of the paragraph.
- The final sentence should return more explicitly to the paragraph's main claim.

## Common diagnosis labels

Use these labels when concise feedback is useful:

- Clear but underdeveloped
- Logically coherent but insufficiently supported
- Strong evidence, weak interpretation
- Good structure, overly strong claims
- Relevant content, unclear paragraph focus
- Appropriate tone, but transitions need refinement
- Useful argument, but topic sentence needs sharpening

## Revision strategy patterns

### If the paragraph lacks focus

Recommended strategy:
1. Identify the main claim.
2. Move that claim to the opening sentence.
3. Remove or relocate sentences that do not support it.
4. Add a concluding sentence that returns to the claim.

### If the paragraph lacks logic

Recommended strategy:
1. Identify the relation between each sentence.
2. Add transitions only where the relation is not obvious.
3. Replace vague transitions with precise logical markers.
4. Add explanatory sentences between evidence and interpretation.

### If the paragraph overclaims

Recommended strategy:
1. Replace causal verbs with associational verbs if needed.
2. Add hedging language.
3. Limit the scope of the claim.
4. Connect the claim to the actual evidence.

## Sample diagnostic comment

The paragraph has a clear general topic, but its analytical focus is not yet explicit. The first two sentences introduce the issue broadly, while the later sentences shift toward implications for policy. To improve coherence, the paragraph should state its central claim earlier and use a clearer transition between the empirical evidence and the policy implication. The tone is mostly academic, although the final claim should be softened because the evidence supports association rather than causation.
